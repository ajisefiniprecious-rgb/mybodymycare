About the Project

Project Questions

"My Body, My Care" is a youth empowerment project that provides menstrual health education and intimate hygiene awareness to adolescent girls (ages 12-18) in rural communities in Ile-Ife, Osun State, Nigeria. It is funded by the Global Youth Mobilization Youth Empowerment Fund.
The project is funded by the Global Youth Mobilization (GYM) through the Youth Empowerment Fund. GYM is a global initiative that supports youth-led projects addressing health and well-being challenges in communities around the world.
We have educated 100 girls and distributed 100 hygiene kits across three secondary schools in rural Ile-Ife. The project will continue until all planned beneficiaries have been reached.
We have visited three secondary schools in rural communities within Ile-Ife, Osun State. We are working to expand our reach to more schools as the project continues.
If you are a school administrator or educator interested in having My Body, My Care visit your school, please contact us through our Contact page. We will review your request and work with you to schedule a visit.

Menstrual Health FAQ

Common questions about menstrual health and hygiene.

Most girls get their first period between ages 10-16, with the average being around 12-13. Factors like genetics, nutrition, and overall health can influence when you start. There is a wide range of "normal," so don't worry if your friends started before or after you.
A typical period lasts between 3-7 days. The flow can vary from light to heavy and may change from day to day. It can take 1-2 years for your cycle to become regular after your first period.
You should change your pad every 4-6 hours, or more often if your flow is heavy. Using the same pad for too long can cause odor, irritation, and increase the risk of infection. Always wash your hands before and after changing your pad.
Yes, mild to moderate cramps are normal and are caused by contractions of the uterus. You can relieve cramps by placing a warm compress on your lower abdomen, gently massaging the area, staying active, or taking a mild pain reliever if needed. If cramps are severe enough to interfere with daily activities, please see a healthcare provider.
Yes! Exercise during your period is perfectly safe and can actually help reduce cramps and improve your mood. Light activities like walking, stretching, or yoga are great options. Listen to your body and do what feels comfortable.
A pad is worn on the outside of the body, attached to underwear, and absorbs menstrual blood as it leaves the body. A tampon is inserted inside the vagina and absorbs blood before it leaves the body. Both are safe to use. Choose whichever option feels more comfortable for you.
